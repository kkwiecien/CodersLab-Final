A few words about me...

My name is Krzysztof Kwiecień, I am a translator, artist, and (most recently) a programmer-in-training, currently living in Warsaw.

I would consider myself something of a reserved person by nature, in that I am reluctant to offer up information that has not been outright requested beforehand, or to engage socially unless engaged first. That said, I certainly do not shy away from conversation topics where I feel I have something to contribute to the discussion. In general, in conversations I will tend to listen more than speak.

Both my education and career path have been rather convoluted - it could be said I am still trying to find my niche in life, or else figure out means to better support at least some of my various interests. I have studied art, language, and programming (though the latter of those with little success prior to my attending the Coders Lab courses). I have been a stocktaking worker in stores large and small, served as a volunteer translator for a university and a museum, and have been working as a translator proper.

As far as leisure goes, I admit to avoiding the busier environments - concerts and clubs are not the place for me. I far prefer exploring a museum at my own pace, or just unwinding somewhere relatively quiet and out of the way. I am also an avid gamer, and a lover of games both in digital form, and of the more traditional boardgame variety.

My dream, I would say, would be to create video games - though whether my role in the process of creation was to be as writer, artist, or programmer... well, I am not too picky in that regard.

About my art...

I generally consider my artistic skill to be something in the range of advanced beginner, or perhaps the lower levels of intermediate. I am by no means a master, but I feel competent in at least the basic elements enough to experiment with technique and subject matter. I have received an artistic education, certainly, but I would not call it a thorough one. As it is, I presently endeavour to watch and study other artists, and to pick up cues from them.

My primary medium is traditional art - pencil sketches, inked lines, and colour pencils. More recently I have started supplementing that with white gel pens for highlights, and even the occasional touch-up through image manipulation software (think Photoshop or GIMP) for effects that would be too difficult or time-consuming to achieve with traditional means. I have also experimented with markers, and have worked with watercolour and oil paint in the past - as well as assorted one-time attempts with some other techniques.

I have also made some forays into the world of digital art, though in my case it is a mixed medium at best. The lines I still do traditionally, but once the inking is done and scanned, I add colour on the computer. Such attempts have mostly been limited to small images - such as avatar icons for use on internet forums.

As far as subject matter goes, my favourite theme would have to be that of anthropomorphic animals (think Bugs Bunny, or Disney's Robin Hood) - I am quite happy to place them in any setting in my art, whether it is high fantasy, modern war, space opera, or cyberpunk. I am also not averse to drawing fan art of media I enjoy, though video games tend to resonate with me the most in that respect, and my fan art gallery here contains examples of fan art of the Mass Effect and XCOM series of games.

My digital art section contains avatars done in a style inspired by the "Order of the Stick" webcomic, and made for members of said comic's forum.

A more complete gallery of my art is available on request.

About my hobbies...

While art remains my main hobby (and occasional occupation), I do have several other pursuits.

First and foremost - fencing, with the distinction that this is historical fencing intended to teach the handling of historical weapons, rather than the olympic sport. Naturally, the training was done with wooden or padded practice weapons, rather than metal - though the latter sort was used by more experienced fencers in practice duels. Personally, I have not quite made the step up to that level just yet, and consider myself a beginner in terms of skill. I have a grasp of the basics, certainly, but it is a long way yet from any real level of skill.

Partly tied to fencing and partly to art is my interest in arms and armour of all sorts, from the relatively modern to the downright ancient. I tend to collect books on the subject, as well as the occasional weapon for display purposes - as nearly all of them would likely not hold up to actual use.

Moving away from fencing, but still tied closely to art is my interest in model collecting - admittedly the models themselves are miniatures meant for tabletop wargaming, but my primary interest there has always been in collecting, assembling, and painting the miniatures, and lack of easy access to partners to play against has precluded me from putting my collection to much use. Still, some of the finer detail work on the models has helped maintain a steady hand, and I even got some practice sculpting additions to the models.

Pictured here is my fencing equipment - a padded jacket, mask, and arm-guards, as well as the weapons used for practice.

The larger weapons are for training longsword techniques - the padded version is an "updated" model, lighter and less dangerous to use, since the completely wooden one has quite a bit of heft to it. The smaller weapon is for sabre training, and while the photo does not show it too well, it has a LOT more wear and tear to it than either longsword. This is due to the fact that while the longsword is my preferred field, I found myself more adept at handling the saber, and as such tended to participate in the training sessions for that far more. The weapons have also been decorated with personal motifs (done personally using ink on wood) to help set them apart from other people's training weapons to help avoid confusion.

Shown here is only a small part of my model collection, though these are the most recent additions, and as such have been worked on with a bit more skill than some of my really early acquisitions.

The largest part of my collection is made up of models from the various wargames published by Games Workshop - though that is primarily due to them being the biggest game in town. The models here are from the Warhammer 40,000 range (specifically the Tau and Imperial Guard armies), though I also have Warhammer Fantasy Battles, Mordheim, and Battlefleet Gothic in my collection. In addition to the games Workshop line, I also have a fondness for the Mutant Chronicles-set wargame, Warzone, and have a small selection of models from that line as well.

While the photo may not show it too well, these most recent models have been personalised quite heavily - the Kroot (on the left) have varying tribal paint, the Fire Warriors (middle) have different squad markings based on their unit, and the Imperial Guardsmen (on the right), in addition to unit markings, each have a name painted on the base, and a gas mask sculpted on out of green stuff (two-part modelling putty).

Shown here are a few pieces from my collection of arms and armour - admitteldy I have much more of the former than the latter.

This particular selection consists of: a (very blunt) katana, the largest piece shown here, a spearhead (in back), a Nepalese kukri (bottom, partly removed from scabbard), and a wooden dagger from Costa Rica the proper name of which I unfortunately do not know. There is also a pair of leather bracers (left) and thick leather gloves (right) - the latter served me for a while during fencing lessons until one too many hand hits convinced me to upgrade to something with a bit more padding. In this particular case it was a pair of motorcycle gloves.
